Traditionally it has long been held that the only correct use of mutual is in describing a reciprocal relationship: mutual respect, for example, means that the parties involved feel respect for each other. The other use of mutual meaning ‘held in common’, as in mutual friend, is held to be incorrect. This latter use has a long and respectable history, however. It was first recorded in Shakespeare, and has since appeared in the writing of Sir Walter Scott, George Eliot, and, most famously, as the title of Dickens's novel Our Mutual Friend. It is now generally accepted as part of standard English
